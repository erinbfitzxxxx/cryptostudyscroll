// THE PROBLEM //
CRYPTO RESEARCH HAS A BLIND SPOT

Most research on cryptocurrency adoption asks one question: does someone own crypto or not? That framing misses something important. Among non-owners, there's a real difference between people who would never consider it and people who would consider owning but haven't yet.

That second group — the considerers — is invisible in most studies. If they behave differently from both rejectors and owners, then lumping them together distorts everything: who we think is open to crypto, what predicts adoption, and what messages or policies might actually move the needle.

We draw on the Theory of Planned Behavior, which holds that people form intentions before acting. Considering a behavior and actually doing it are distinct stages — and the factors that move someone from rejection to consideration may be completely different from what moves them from consideration to ownership.

OUR ARGUMENT → CONSIDERERS AND OWNERS BOTH DIFFER FROM REJECTORS — BUT CONSIDERERS AREN'T NECESSARILY LIKE OWNERS IN ALL WAYS OR TO THE SAME DEGREE.

// THE METHOD //
HOW WE TESTED IT

We used a modeling approach that simultaneously estimates the probability of being a rejector, considerer, or owner. We repeated this across three independent samples — different people, different timing, different survey providers — to ensure reliability.

Study 1 — Cooperative Election Study (Oct–Dec 2024, n = 803)
Study 2 — Lucid (Feb–Mar 2025, n = 1,908)

// THE HEADLINE //
IN ALL THREE SAMPLES,
CONSIDERERS AND OWNERS ≠ REJECTORS
BUT CONSIDERERS ≠ OWNERS
— not in all ways, or to the same degree
Compared to rejectors, both considerers and owners skew younger, male, higher income, less religious, more open to experience, less conscientious, and more risk-tolerant. But some predictors separate the two adoption stages from each other — and those distinctions matter for understanding where adoption stalls.

// SO WHAT? //
WHAT OUR FINDINGS MEAN FOR...
// FOR ADVOCATES, INDUSTRY, AND COMMUNICATORS

Understanding who considers — and what moves them from intentions to action — has direct implications for product design, onboarding, and communication strategies. Whether one's goal is to endorse or denounce crypto, the "considerer" is arguably the most actionable audience: potentially open to adoption, but hasn't taken action yet.

Our work suggests no single approach will appeal to all. Efforts to reduce uncertainty and promote legitimacy may attract considerers, but backfire with owners who think differently about institutional disruption and risk.

// FOR POLICYMAKERS

That roughly 20% of US adults would consider owning crypto suggests significant latent demand. Consideration may depend on pending regulation, perceived risk, and legitimacy signals, including endorsement from trusted elites. Policymakers will likely need to balance both mainstream and alternative frames.

// FOR RESEARCHERS

Looking only at ownership obscures variation among non-owners. By accounting for considerers, we find certain factors (e.g., ideology and tech attitudes) do matter for crypto adoption — just not for the ownership stage. Our approach also helps determine when other factors (e.g., Need for Chaos) matter in the process, providing direction for future research.

// FOR ADOPTION DIFFUSION

Considering and owning rates from all three studies suggest US crypto adoption is at or nearing the "early majority" stage. We don't yet know how the landscape will evolve, or whether the motivations of the next wave of adopters will look the same. Regardless, our findings align with decades of attitude-behavior research and should be broadly applicable.

// FOR BITCOINERS

Most respondents would consider or own Bitcoin, but a sizable share have multiple types and/or don't engage with Bitcoin at all. Limiting analysis to Bitcoin ignores part of the crypto-engaged population and makes it virtually impossible to disentangle asset-specific relationships.

We tested for, but did not find, asset-specific differences in our data. Our multi-study evidence suggests this is not currently the case — though it's possible such differences could emerge.
